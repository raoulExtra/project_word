# Quantification in Logic

## Definition
Quantification in logic is the formal process of specifying how many elements satisfy a given predicate or property, using logical operators to express universal and existential claims about domains. For general treatment in formal science, see [quantification](sciences_ot_formal/_/q/quantification.md).

## Details
- **Purpose**: Express scope and extent over sets of objects in formal systems
- **Key aspects**:
  - Universal quantification (∀x: P(x) = "for all x, P(x) holds")
  - Existential quantification (∃x: P(x) = "there exists at least one x where P(x) holds")
  - Negation of quantifiers (¬∀ and ¬∃)
- **Types**:
  - Universal quantifier (∀, "for all", "every")
  - Existential quantifier (∃, "there exists", "some")
  - Restricted quantification (domain-limited)
- **Role**: Fundamental to first-order logic and mathematical reasoning, enabling precise expression of general truths

## Context
In formal logic, quantification allows the formulation of universal laws and existence claims. It is essential for constructing valid arguments, proving theorems, and defining properties across entire domains or subsets thereof. Quantifiers are among the most basic logical operators alongside conjunction, disjunction, and implication.
