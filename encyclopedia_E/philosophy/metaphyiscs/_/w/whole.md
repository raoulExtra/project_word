# Whole

## Definition
Whole refers to a complete entity or structure that contains parts, understood in terms of mereological relationships. It denotes the unified entity formed by the fusion of its constituent elements, whether physical, abstract, or conceptual.

## Details
- **Purpose**: Analyze the nature of completeness and the relationships between parts and their unifying structures
- **Key aspects**:
  - Mereological sum (the whole as sum of parts)
  - Fusion (combining parts into a unified whole)
  - Completeness (having all requisite parts)
  - Unity (the binding together of diverse elements)
- **Types**:
  - Physical whole (material object with spatial extent)
  - Abstract whole (conceptual or mathematical unity)
  - Temporal whole (extended in time as a unified sequence)
  - Modal whole (complete in all relevant aspects)
- **Role**: Essential to mereology, metaphysical composition problems, and the analysis of identity and persistence

## Context
In metaphysics, the whole-part relationship is fundamental to understanding how complexes are built from simples and how identity persists through change. The nature of wholes raises questions about material constitution, temporal parts, and the criteria for mereological completeness.
