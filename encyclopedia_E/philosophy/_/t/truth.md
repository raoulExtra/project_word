# Truth

## Definition
Truth is the property of being the case, corresponding to reality, or accurately representing facts about the world. It is the fundamental norm for beliefs, statements, and theories.

## Details
- **Purpose**: Establish criteria for what makes beliefs, statements, or theories accurate representations of reality
- **Key aspects**:
  - Correspondence (matching reality)
  - Coherence (consistency within a system)
  - Pragmatic (useful consequences)
  - Performative (successful action)
- **Types**:
  - Factual truth (statements about the world)
  - Logical truth (true by form, e.g., tautologies)
  - Epistemic truth (what is known to be true)
  - Aesthetic truth (artistic or moral correctness)
- **Role**: Central to epistemology, logic, ethics, and the philosophy of language

## Context
In philosophy, truth is studied across multiple traditions. The correspondence theory (truth as matching reality) dominates analytic philosophy, while coherence theories (truth as consistency within systems) are prominent in holistic and German philosophical traditions. Pragmatic theories (truth as useful) emerge from pragmatist philosophers like Peirce and James.
